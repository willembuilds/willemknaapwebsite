02How I work

How I work inside a mandate.

Strategy is usually not the hardest part. What decides the outcome is the operating system underneath it: who owns what, how decisions get made, and the rhythm that turns intent into delivery.

01

Assess and frame

I start from where the organisation actually is: the ambition, constraints, stakeholders, interests and where value can genuinely sit. Sometimes from a blank page, sometimes in the middle of the chaos.

02

Design the operating model

Objectives, governance, decision rights and roles, with the data and AI landscape underneath. The structure that makes delivery possible.

03

Select the use cases

Choose where value is real and provable. Define and prioritise the use cases, so time, money and attention go to what matters, not to oversized solutions without a business case.

04

Build, test and align

Build frameworks, ways of working and first deliverables around a clear goal. Programs run on people, so I align interests, ownership, decision-making and timing while the work is being built.

05

Adopt, scale and embed

From pilot to production, embedded in teams with feedback loops, clear ownership and a careful handover.

06

Learn, change, adapt

Every project, program and pilot teaches the organisation something. I make sure that learning is captured, embedded and put to work, instead of lost when the work ends.

Every engagement runs as a clearly defined assignment, with agreed objectives, governance and deliverables. I work independently, bring my own methodologies and tooling, and focus on building lasting capability inside the organisation.

My methodology

The TMM6 Framework

Complex transformations require more than project management. TMM6 is how I run them: trust, mandate and momentum, combined with governance, executive alignment and structured delivery to accelerate complex transformations.

The framework is continuously developed and validated in practice. Part of that is the TMM6 app: not a survey, but an LLM-driven engine that turns your input into concrete advice, while the model validates itself and learns from every interaction: a self-improving loop. More at tmm6.ai.

04Perspective

Where perspective becomes execution.

History is context. The better you understand where something comes from, the sharper you can see what it can become. That is how I look at art and music, and ultimately also how I look at organisations, programs and AI.

Where I learned to look

Art teaches observation

I have gone to museums since I was a child. I grew up around ateliers, second-hand bookshops and exhibitions, with a father who is an art historian, and now pass that on to my own children.

In front of a work, I look at composition, material, craft, choices and omissions. Those things say something about the work and about the maker. I read a project, team or organisation in a similar way: not only what is there, but why it came to be, what is missing, and what it would take to move it forward.

01Art
What I listen for

Music teaches movement

Music works the same way for me. I do not only hear the song. I listen to the writing, composition, voice, performance, production and the influences underneath it.

From Vivaldi and Robert Johnson, through Charlie Parker, Elvis, The Beatles and Hendrix, to Springsteen, Michael Jackson, Nirvana, Coldplay, Kendrick Lamar and David Guetta: almost nothing comes from nowhere. Where a movement starts often tells you where it can still go.

Sometimes it is simply about enjoying craft and originality. Sometimes it is about recognising when something is genuinely new, for a large audience or for a small community. That same attention is what I bring to work: influence, timing, context and execution.

02Music
Craft turns perspective into execution

None of this is decoration. Art and music keep me sharp on the things my work runs on: reading context, forming a point of view, recognising craft and making something actually work.

Ideas are not enough. Strategy is not enough either. The value is in understanding where something comes from, seeing what it can become, and then building it in the real world: organisations, programs, ventures and AI adoption.
